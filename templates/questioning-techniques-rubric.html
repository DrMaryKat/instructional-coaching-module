Questioning Techniques Rubric

Supporting K-3 Teachers in Developing Critical Thinking Through Questions

Purpose

This rubric supports instructional coaches in observing and providing feedback on questioning techniques in K-3 classrooms. It focuses on helping teachers move beyond recall questions to develop students' critical thinking skills through strategic questioning.

How to Use This Rubric

  • Use during classroom observations to assess current questioning practices
  • Share with teachers during pre-conferences to set goals
  • Reference during coaching conversations to provide specific feedback
  • Track progress over time by dating observations

Questioning Techniques Rubric

Criteria Beginning (1) Developing (2) Proficient (3) Exemplary (4)
Question Complexity (DOK Levels) Primarily asks Level 1 recall questions (who, what, when, where) Includes some Level 2 questions (how, why) but mostly Level 1 Balances Levels 1-3, with intentional progression Strategically uses all DOK levels, including Level 4 extended thinking
Wait Time Provides 0-1 seconds before calling on students or answering own question Sometimes pauses 2-3 seconds, inconsistent Consistently provides 3-5 seconds wait time Varies wait time based on complexity; teaches students to use thinking time
Student Engagement Calls on volunteers only; same students answer Attempts to call on various students but patterns exist Uses strategies to engage all students (sticks, think-pair-share) All students actively engaged through varied participation structures
Follow-Up Questions Accepts answers without probing; moves to next question Occasionally asks "why?" or "how do you know?" Regularly probes thinking with follow-up questions Skillfully builds on responses to deepen understanding
Cultural Responsiveness Questions lack connection to students' lives/experiences Some attempts to connect to student experiences Questions regularly connect to students' cultures and lives Questions honor and build on cultural knowledge and experiences
Scaffolding No differentiation; same questions for all Some attempts to modify questions for struggling students Strategically scaffolds questions based on student needs Expertly differentiates questions while maintaining rigor

Examples of Effective Questioning Sequences

Example 1: Reading - "The Three Little Pigs"

  • Level 1: "What did the first pig use to build his house?" (straw)
  • Level 2: "Why do you think the third pig chose bricks?" (stronger, learned from others)
  • Level 3: "If you were a pig, what material would you choose and why?"
  • Level 4: "Design a house that would keep all three pigs safe. What would you need?"

Cultural Connection: "What materials do people in our neighborhood use to build strong homes?"

Example 2: Math - Addition Within 20

  • Level 1: "What is 8 + 5?" (13)
  • Level 2: "How did you figure out 8 + 5? Show me your strategy."
  • Level 3: "Can you create a word problem that equals 13?"
  • Level 4: "How many different ways can you make 13? Create a pattern."

Cultural Connection: "If you're at the corner store with $13, what could you buy?"

Example 3: Science - Plant Growth

  • Level 1: "What parts of a plant can you name?" (roots, stem, leaves)
  • Level 2: "How are seeds and bulbs different?"
  • Level 3: "What would happen if we put one plant in the closet?"
  • Level 4: "Design an experiment to see what plants need to grow."

Cultural Connection: "What plants does your family grow at home or like to eat?"

Effective Questioning Strategies

Talk Moves to Extend Thinking

  • Revoicing: "So you're saying that..." (clarifies and validates)
  • Reasoning: "Why do you think that?" (probes deeper)
  • Adding On: "Who can add to Maria's idea?" (builds community)
  • Turn & Talk: "Tell your partner what you think" (increases participation)
  • Wait Time: "I'm going to give everyone thinking time" (normalizes pausing)

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Rapid-fire questioning: Not allowing time for deep thinking
  • Guess what I'm thinking: Looking for one "right" answer only
  • Ignoring incorrect answers: Missing teaching opportunities
  • Complex language: Using vocabulary beyond students' comprehension
  • Closed questioning patterns: Only yes/no or single-word responses

Coaching Conversation Starters

After Observation

  • "I noticed you asked 15 questions in that lesson. Let's look at the types..."
  • "Your students were really engaged when you asked about their experiences with..."
  • "What did you notice about student responses when you provided wait time?"
  • "Let's think about how we could push that question to a higher DOK level..."
  • "Tell me about your questioning plan for this lesson."
