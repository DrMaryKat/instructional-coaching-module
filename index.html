Case Study: Washington Elementary-Middle School

School Profile:

  • Recently merged K-8 school in an impoverished inner city neighborhood
  • 500 students (82% African American from low-income homes)
  • 24 teachers (79% white, 16 on improvement plans)
  • Middle school teachers (grades 4-8) lack foundational literacy knowledge
  • K-3 teachers struggle with critical questioning techniques

Training Objectives

By the end of this module, instructional coaches will be able to:

  • Analyze the complex dynamics of merged schools in urban settings
  • Build teacher capacity in foundational literacy concepts
  • Implement critical questioning techniques across grade levels
  • Foster culturally responsive teaching practices
  • Create sustainable professional development plans
  • Support teachers on improvement plans with dignity and effectiveness

Module 1: Understanding the Context

The Complexity of Merged Schools

When schools merge, they bring together distinct cultures, expectations, and pedagogical approaches. This creates both challenges and opportunities for instructional coaches.

Key Considerations:

  • Cultural Dynamics: 82% African American student population with 79% white teaching staff
  • Economic Context: Low-income community with specific educational needs
  • Professional Development Needs: 67% of teachers on improvement plans
  • Grade-Level Divide: Elementary vs. middle school pedagogical approaches

Activity 1.1: Context Mapping

Create a visual map of the school's current state. Consider:

  • Teacher strengths and areas for growth
  • Student demographic and academic data
  • Resource availability and constraints
  • Community assets and partnerships

Building Trust in Challenging Circumstances

With 16 teachers on improvement plans, establishing trust is crucial. Approach coaching with:

  • Asset-based mindset: Identify and build on existing strengths
  • Non-evaluative stance: Position yourself as a collaborative partner
  • Cultural humility: Acknowledge and address cultural dynamics
  • Transparency: Be clear about goals and processes

Reflection Questions

  1. How might the cultural mismatch between teachers and students impact classroom dynamics?
  2. What assumptions might teachers hold that need to be addressed?
  3. How can you leverage community knowledge and assets?

Module 2: Building Literacy Knowledge for Middle School Teachers

The Challenge

Middle school teachers (grades 4-8) report not understanding basic literacy terminology like "grapheme." This knowledge gap impacts their ability to support struggling readers.

Essential Literacy Concepts

1. Phonological and Phonemic Awareness

Term Definition Example Why It Matters
Phoneme The smallest unit of sound /c/ /a/ /t/ in "cat" Foundation for decoding
Grapheme Letter or letters representing a phoneme "ch" represents /ch/ Connects sound to print
Morpheme Smallest unit of meaning "un-" means "not" Supports vocabulary growth

2. The Simple View of Reading

Reading Comprehension = Decoding Ã— Language Comprehension

  • Both components are essential
  • Middle school students may struggle with either or both
  • Assessment must identify specific needs

Activity 2.1: Literacy Concept Carousel

Design a professional development session where teachers rotate through stations to learn key literacy concepts:

  1. Station 1: Phoneme-Grapheme Mapping
  2. Station 2: Morphology in Action
  3. Station 3: Fluency Building Strategies
  4. Station 4: Comprehension Scaffolds

Adolescent Literacy Strategies

Evidence-Based Approaches for Grades 4-8:

  • Word Study: Systematic morphology instruction
  • Fluency Practice: Repeated reading with purpose
  • Vocabulary Development: Explicit instruction in academic language
  • Comprehension Strategies: Before, during, and after reading activities

Module 3: Developing Critical Questioning Techniques

The Power of Questions in Learning

K-3 teachers at Washington Elementary-Middle School need support in using critical questioning techniques that promote deeper thinking and engagement.

Webb's Depth of Knowledge Framework

DOK Level Description Question Stems Example
Level 1: Recall Basic recall of facts Who? What? When? Where? "What happened first in the story?"
Level 2: Skill/Concept Using information How? Why? Compare... "How are these two characters alike?"
Level 3: Strategic Thinking Reasoning and planning What if? Justify... Create... "What would happen if the character made a different choice?"
Level 4: Extended Thinking Complex reasoning over time Design... Synthesize... Evaluate... "Create a new ending and explain how it changes the story's message"

Wait Time and Talk Moves

Essential Practices:

  • Wait Time 1: 3-5 seconds after asking a question
  • Wait Time 2: 3-5 seconds after a student responds
  • Talk Moves:
    • Revoicing: "So you're saying..."
    • Repeating: Ask students to repeat
    • Reasoning: "Why do you think that?"
    • Adding on: "Who can add to this idea?"
    • Wait time: Strategic pauses

Coaching Conversation Starters

  • "I noticed you asked mostly Level 1 questions. What might happen if we added some Level 3 questions?"
  • "Your students had great ideas. How could we give them more time to develop their thoughts?"
  • "Let's think about questions that connect to your students' neighborhood experiences..."

Module 4: Culturally Responsive Teaching Practices

Addressing the Cultural Gap

With 82% African American students and 79% white teachers, culturally responsive teaching is essential for student success.

Zaretta Hammond's Culturally Responsive Teaching Framework

The Ready for Rigor Framework

  • Awareness: Understanding one's own cultural lens
  • Learning Partnerships: Building authentic relationships
  • Information Processing: How culture impacts learning
  • Community of Learners: Creating inclusive environments

Practical Strategies for Teachers

1. Mirror, Window, and Sliding Glass Door Books

  • Mirrors: Books reflecting students' own experiences
  • Windows: Books showing different experiences
  • Sliding Glass Doors: Books students can step into

2. Cultural Learning Styles

Collectivist Cultures Individualist Cultures Classroom Implications
Group harmony valued Individual achievement valued Balance both approaches
Collaborative learning Independent work Offer choice in grouping
Oral traditions strong Written traditions emphasized Include storytelling and discussion

Activity 4.1: Cultural Asset Mapping

Help teachers identify cultural assets in their students and community:

  1. Language practices (including home languages and dialects)
  2. Family and community knowledge
  3. Cultural traditions and celebrations
  4. Problem-solving approaches
  5. Communication styles

Addressing Implicit Bias

Reflective Questions for Teachers:

  • What assumptions do I make about students based on appearance or behavior?
  • How do my expectations differ for different groups of students?
  • What cultural norms am I enforcing that may not align with students' home cultures?
  • How can I learn more about my students' communities and experiences?

Module 5: Creating Sustainable Change Through Coaching

Developing a Comprehensive Coaching Plan

Phase 1: Assessment and Relationship Building (Weeks 1-4)

  • Conduct classroom observations using non-evaluative protocols
  • Hold individual conferences with all teachers
  • Administer literacy knowledge assessment for middle school teachers
  • Survey K-3 teachers on questioning techniques comfort level
  • Build relationships through informal interactions

Phase 2: Targeted Professional Development (Weeks 5-12)

Week K-3 Focus 4-8 Focus Whole School
5-6 DOK questioning levels Phoneme-grapheme basics Cultural responsiveness intro
7-8 Wait time & talk moves Morphology instruction Implicit bias workshop
9-10 Culturally responsive questions Comprehension strategies Community partnerships
11-12 Integration & practice Assessment & intervention Celebration & planning

Phase 3: Coaching Cycles (Ongoing)

The Coaching Cycle Process

  1. Pre-Conference: Set goals and plan together
  2. Observation: Gather data on agreed focus area
  3. Post-Conference: Reflect and plan next steps
  4. Follow-Up: Check in and provide resources

For teachers on improvement plans, increase frequency and provide additional support between cycles.

Supporting Teachers on Improvement Plans

Differentiated Support Strategies:

  • Weekly check-ins: Brief, supportive conversations
  • Co-teaching opportunities: Model strategies in their classroom
  • Peer observations: Arrange visits to strong practitioners
  • Resource curation: Provide targeted materials and tools
  • Celebration of growth: Document and acknowledge progress
